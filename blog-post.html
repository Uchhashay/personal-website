What the Epstein Files teach us when power scales

The Epstein Files are disturbing not just for what happened, but for what they reveal about how power behaves once it scales beyond ordinary rules.

Nietzsche argued that morality is not universal, it is contextual.

The Jeffrey Epstein case unsettles me deeply for what happened, and for what it reveals about how differently reality operates at extreme levels of wealth, access, and network density.

Rules don’t disappear at the top.
They mutate.

They become slower.
Procedural.
Negotiable.
Delayed.

And most importantly,
collective.

No single villain.
No single hero.
Just a web where responsibility scatters so efficiently that outrage has nowhere precise to land.

This isn’t a defense of power.
It’s an observation of how systems behave once they cross a certain threshold.

If you don’t understand how power actually works at this level,
you’ll either romanticize it or resent it.

Both are dangerous positions.

The real skill is seeing clearly, without illusion.
